    Mabel Dodge Luhan House¶

    Basic Information¶

    Field Value
    Official Name Mabel Dodge Luhan House
    Other Names Big House, Los Gallos
    Type Cultural/Historical
    Subtype Historic residence, cultural salon, hotel/conference center
    Location 240 Morada Lane, Taos, New Mexico
    Coordinates 36.4072°N, 105.5731°W
    Elevation/Dimensions ~7,000 feet elevation; large adobe compound
    Designation National Historic Landmark (1991)
    Managing Authority Private ownership (operates as hotel and conference center)
    Accessibility Public (as hotel/conference center; tours available)

    Overview¶

    The Mabel Dodge Luhan House stands as one of the most significant cultural landmarks in Taos—a National Historic Landmark that served as the epicenter of the Taos art colony and a cultural salon that attracted major artists, writers, and intellectuals from around the world. [UNUSUAL] Located at 240 Morada Lane in Taos, the house represents an early example of Pueblo Revival architecture, blending traditional Pueblo and Spanish Colonial elements in a design that would influence regional architecture for decades.

    The house's significance extends far beyond its architectural achievement. [TURNING] Mabel Dodge Luhan's 1917 arrival in Taos and her establishment of a cultural salon here marked a pivotal moment in the region's transformation from a remote mountain town to an internationally recognized cultural destination. Her ability to attract figures including D.H. Lawrence, Georgia O'Keeffe, Ansel Adams, and countless others created a cultural bridge between East Coast intellectual circles and the American Southwest.

    Today, the Mabel Dodge Luhan House operates as a hotel and conference center, preserving its historic character while continuing its legacy as a gathering place for artists, writers, and cultural figures. [HUMAN] This dual identity—as both preserved historic site and active cultural center—demonstrates how places of cultural significance can evolve while maintaining their essential character. The house's continued operation as a cultural gathering place honors Luhan's vision while adapting to contemporary needs.

    Physical Description¶

    Appearance¶

    The Mabel Dodge Luhan House is a large adobe compound built in the Pueblo Revival style, blending traditional Pueblo architectural elements with Spanish Colonial influences. [UNUSUAL] The house features thick adobe walls, vigas (exposed wooden beams), flat roofs, and traditional details that create a distinctive regional aesthetic. The compound's scale and sophistication demonstrate Luhan's vision of creating a cultural center that would attract major artistic and intellectual figures.

    The house's layout reflects both residential needs and its function as a cultural salon. [HUMAN] Large common spaces, guest rooms, and gathering areas were designed to accommodate the artists, writers, and intellectuals who visited. The property's grounds, including gardens and outdoor spaces, provided additional areas for cultural exchange and creative collaboration.

    • Elevation: ~7,000 feet above sea level
    • Notable Features: Pueblo Revival architecture, adobe construction, vigas, traditional details, large compound layout
    • Built Structures: Main house, guest accommodations, outbuildings, gardens

    Notable Features¶

    The house's most distinctive features include its Pueblo Revival architecture, the scale and sophistication of the adobe construction, and the layout designed for cultural gatherings. [UNUSUAL] The house represents an early and influential example of Pueblo Revival style, blending traditional regional architecture with modern needs. The property's continued use as a cultural gathering place maintains its original function while adapting to contemporary purposes.

    Changes Over Time¶

    While maintaining essential architectural and cultural character, the Mabel Dodge Luhan House has adapted to changing circumstances. [TURNING] The property's transition from private residence to hotel and conference center represents an evolution that preserves its cultural function while making it accessible to new generations. The house has incorporated modern amenities while maintaining its historic character and architectural integrity.

    Construction/Creation¶

    • Date: Built between 1917 and 1922
    • Builder/Creator: Mabel Dodge Luhan; designed in collaboration with architects and builders
    • Purpose: Private residence and cultural salon; gathering place for artists, writers, and intellectuals
    • Methods/Materials: Adobe construction (mud, straw, water); Pueblo Revival style; traditional regional building methods adapted for modern needs

    The house's construction represents Luhan's vision of creating a cultural center that would attract major artistic and intellectual figures.     Historical Events [TURNING]¶

    1917: Luhan Arrives in Taos Mabel Dodge Luhan moved to Taos in 1917, fleeing New York society and seeking "authentic" indigenous culture and remote isolation. [TURNING] Her arrival marked the beginning of Taos's transformation into an international cultural destination, as she began attracting major artists and writers to the region.

    1918: Marriage to Tony Lujan Luhan's 1918 marriage to Tony Lujan (Antonio Mirabal Lujan), a Taos Pueblo member, created a remarkable cross-cultural bridge. [TURNING] This marriage reflected both the opportunities and complexities of cultural exchange, connecting Luhan to the Pueblo community while establishing her place in Taos's cultural landscape.

    1917-1922: House Construction The house was built between 1917 and 1922, designed to serve as both residence and cultural salon. [TURNING] The construction period coincided with Luhan's establishment of her cultural salon and her efforts to attract major figures to Taos.

    1922-1925: D.H. Lawrence Residence D.H. Lawrence stayed at the house during his Taos period (1922-1925), writing about the region and contributing to its literary reputation. [TURNING] Lawrence's residence here, facilitated by Luhan, added to the house's significance as a literary landmark.

    1929: Georgia O'Keeffe Visits Georgia O'Keeffe visited Taos in 1929, staying with Luhan and beginning her connection to northern New Mexico. [TURNING] O'Keeffe's visit, facilitated by Luhan's cultural network, contributed to the house's role in connecting major artists to the Southwest.

    1962: Luhan's Death Mabel Dodge Luhan died in Taos in 1962, having lived at the house for 45 years. [TURNING] Her death marked the end of an era, though the house's cultural legacy continued through its preservation and continued use.

    1991: National Historic Landmark Designation The house was designated a National Historic Landmark in 1991, recognizing its significance to American cultural history. [TURNING] This designation brought official recognition to the house's role in Taos's cultural development and its association with major artistic and literary figures.

    D.H. Lawrence (1885-1930) Lawrence's residence at the house during his Taos period (1922-1925) added to its literary significance. [HUMAN] His writings about Taos, produced during his stay, document the region and contribute to the house's place in literary history.

    Georgia O'Keeffe (1887-1986) O'Keeffe's 1929 visit, facilitated by Luhan, began her connection to northern New Mexico. [HUMAN] Her presence at the house, along with other major artists, contributed to the cultural salon's significance and Taos's artistic reputation.

    Ansel Adams (1902-1984) Adams visited the house and Taos, photographing the region and contributing to its visual documentation. [HUMAN] His presence, along with other major photographers and artists, added to the house's role as a cultural gathering place.
