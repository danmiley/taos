    Writing Directives¶

    Common to all essays

    Target Length: 4,000–6,000 Words¶

    Each essay should aim for 4,000–6,000 words—the length of a substantial magazine feature or long-form essay in Orion or The Atlantic.

    This length enables: - McPhee patience without rushing geological or historical time - Solnit spiral with a real return at the end - Consistent voice maintained throughout - The "twist" properly built toward

    Essays under 3,000 words will feel compressed; over 7,000 should be split.

    The Solnit Method¶

    Begin with a specific object, place, or fact, then spiral outward into unexpected connections. Allow digression to become argument.

    McPhee's Patience¶

    Don't rush geological or historical time. Let the reader feel the weight of millennia.

    Core Principles¶

    Principle Application
    Seek the counterintuitive The best essay will argue against received wisdom while remaining grounded in evidence
    Honor specificity Names, dates, species, rock types. The universal emerges from the particular
    End with an open door Essays should leave readers with a new question, not a closed conclusion

    Narrative Weighting¶

    Apply project narrative flags where appropriate: - [UNEXPECTED] — Facts contradicting common assumptions - [PARADOX] — Details reconciling conflicting accounts - [UNUSUAL] — Outliers, anomalies, statistical rarities - [HUMAN] — Personal stories, emotional stakes, character details - [TURNING] — Moments where outcomes hung in balance

    Essay Categories¶

    History Essays¶

    Essays exploring the region's human past through unexpected angles: - Infrastructure paradoxes (roads, bridges, utilities that shaped culture) - Failed projects that defined what the region became by not happening - Succession of human communities and their relationships - Lost practices and their consequences

    Themes Essays¶

    Thematic explorations of ecology, geology, and culture: - Natural phenomena as protagonists (fog, fire, earthquakes, tides) - Species stories across deep time - Geological biography and landscape change - Climate and its cultural effects

    Perspectives Essays¶

    Comparative and philosophical examinations: - The region in conversation with similar landscapes globally - What makes this place different from its geological siblings - Philosophical questions raised by the landscape - Outside perspectives on regional identity

    Example Essay Types¶

    These examples illustrate the kinds of essays that work well for regional projects. Adapt to your specific region's characteristics.

    Infrastructure Paradox Essay¶

    Pattern: A project intended to do X actually accomplished the opposite of X.

    Example prompt structure: "Trace the paradox of [infrastructure project]—how a [era] project designed to '[stated goal]' became, through [mechanism], the instrument of [opposite outcome]. Follow the chain: [step 1] brings [step 2], [step 2] brings [step 3], [step 3] breeds [result]. Consider how [characteristic instability or challenge] functions as a kind of [metaphor] on [larger pattern]."

    Species Biography Essay¶

    Pattern: Tell a species' story across multiple timescales to reveal something about the landscape.

    Example prompt structure: "Write [species]'s story across three timescales. First, [deep time abundance]—when [historical context]. Then the slow contraction: how [cause] began a [timeframe] squeeze. Finally, the [modern era] crisis and [current state]. The twist: argue that [current population] represents not a 'return to [idealized state]' but something unprecedented—[specific characteristics that make it new]."

    Hermit Sequence Essay¶

    Pattern: Map succession of people who chose the region for specific reasons, revealing something about place as thinking technology.

    Example prompt structure: "Map the succession of [type of person] who used [region]'s [characteristic] as a [metaphor for function]: [Person A] doing [activity] in the [era], [Person B] arriving [circumstances] in [year] and producing [work], [Person/group C] [activity] in [year]. Argue that [region] functioned as a '[metaphor]' where [pattern] was developed before being transmitted to [elsewhere]."

    Fire/Climate Regime Essay¶

    Pattern: Three-era structure showing how a natural process was managed, suppressed, and is now returning in transformed form.

    Example prompt structure: "Investigate the lost [regime] of the [indigenous people]—the regular, intentional [activity] that shaped [region]'s [features] for millennia. Then trace the [timeframe] experiment in [suppression] and its consequences. The surprising argument: the '[idealized state]' that conservationists sought to protect was itself a human artifact. What does it mean to preserve something that was never unmanaged?"

    Geological Sibling Essay¶

    Pattern: Place region in global conversation with similar landscapes to understand what makes it distinctive.

    Example prompt structure: "Place [region] in planetary conversation with its geological siblings: [similar place 1], [similar place 2], [similar place 3]. Investigate what happens when [geological process]—the specific drama of [tectonic/climate context]. But push beyond geology: why did some of these [landscapes] become [one outcome] while [region] remained [different outcome]? What combination of [factor 1], [factor 2], and [factor 3] made [region] distinctive?"

    Atmospheric Phenomenon Essay¶

    Pattern: Treat a climate/weather feature as protagonist, tracing its physics, cultural meaning, and uncertain future.

    Example prompt structure: "Treat [phenomenon] not as backdrop but as protagonist. Trace the physics—[technical description]. Then explore its cultural work: how [phenomenon] made [region] [quality] to [group], how it [ecological function], how it became [cultural signature]. Consider [phenomenon]'s history—how it [economic/social effects]. End with climate change: [phenomenon] is measurably [changing]. What happens to a landscape defined by [characteristic] when the [characteristic] [changes]?"

    Failure Archive Essay¶

    Pattern: Catalog projects that didn't happen to reveal how refusal shaped regional character.

    Example prompt structure: "Catalog the projects that didn't happen: the [era] plan to [project 1], the various [type] schemes that collapsed, the proposed [project 2], the [era] [project 3] that would have [transformation]. Argue that [region]'s character was shaped as much by what failed as by what succeeded—that the region is a museum of thwarted ambition. Consider how [economic factor] combined with [social factor] to create a landscape defined by refusal."

    Writing Models¶

    These works exemplify the essay style we're pursuing:

    • McPhee, John, Annals of the Former World (1998) — Model for geological patience
    • McPhee, John, The Control of Nature (1989) — Landscape as antagonist
    • Solnit, Rebecca, A Field Guide to Getting Lost (2005) — Spiraling from specific to universal
    • Solnit, Rebecca, Wanderlust: A History of Walking (2000) — History through unconventional lens
    • Solnit, Rebecca, Savage Dreams: A Journey into the Landscape Wars of the American West (1994) — Model for landscape essay writing
    • Lopez, Barry, Arctic Dreams (1986) — Place as subject, not backdrop
    • Dillard, Annie, Pilgrim at Tinker Creek (1974) — Natural observation as meditation

    Workflow¶

    Essay Creation Process¶

    1. Research Phase
    2. Gather sources from bibliography and project docs
    3. Identify the "specific object" to begin with
    4. Map the spiral: where will the essay expand to?

    5. Identify the counterintuitive twist

    6. Draft Phase

    7. Write in drafts/essays/[category]/
    8. Follow essay template structure
    9. Apply narrative weighting flags
    10. Include inline references

    11. Target 4,000-6,000 words

    12. Bibliography Phase

    13. Curate 5-10 accessible sources
    14. Write brief annotations for each

    15. Include project cross-references

    16. Self-Review

    17. Check against writing directives
    18. Verify all facts against sources

    19. Ensure argument is counterintuitive but grounded

    20. Human Review

    21. Submit for review
    22. Address feedback

    23. Revise as needed

    24. Publication

    25. Move to docs/essays/[category]/
    26. Update README index
    27. Begin next essay only after approval

    Review Checklist¶

    • Opens with specific object/fact/place
    • Spirals outward into unexpected connections
    • Contains counterintuitive central argument
    • Honors specificity (names, dates, species)
    • Falls within 4,000-6,000 word target
    • Ends with open question, not closed conclusion
    • Bibliography is accessible to general readers
    • Cross-references project documentation
    • Narrative flags applied appropriately
