    Taos, New Mexico - Deep Time: Evolutionary History¶

    Overview¶

    Taos, New Mexico presents a fundamental paleontological paradox: life has existed here for hundreds of millions of years—dinosaurs walked where Wheeler Peak now stands, mammoths likely grazed in the valley, and marine creatures swam in Paleozoic seas—yet the region's fossil record is remarkably sparse. [PARADOX] The explanation lies in what we might call "The Basalt Barrier and Erosion Story": Precambrian metamorphic and igneous rocks that crystallized deep underground, Cenozoic volcanic basalt flows that buried older deposits, and extensive erosion that has stripped away fossil-bearing sediments. [UNEXPECTED]

    Despite these challenges, Taos preserves evidence of life spanning hundreds of millions of years, from Paleozoic marine organisms in limestone foothills to the Pleistocene megafauna that roamed these mountains just 12,000 years ago. The region's evolutionary story culminates in the high-elevation ecosystems of today—biological communities adapted to extreme elevation gradients, compressed life zones, and dramatic climate swings that have shaped both ancient and modern life.

    Understanding Taos's evolutionary history requires thinking not just about what lived here, but about how geology has preserved—or erased—the evidence. The region's fossil record is fragmentary, but proxy records—tree rings, glacial features, pollen cores, and living fossils—fill critical gaps, revealing a story of dramatic environmental change, species persistence, and evolutionary adaptation.

    Narrative Highlights¶

    Flag Evolutionary Story
    [PARADOX] Dinosaurs walked here for 186 million years, yet no dinosaur fossils have been definitively found—the basalt flows that buried older deposits and extensive erosion have erased the evidence
    [UNEXPECTED] The oldest rocks (1.7-1.8 BYA Precambrian basement) are exposed at New Mexico's highest elevations—rocks that formed deep underground now tower 13,000+ feet above sea level
    [UNUSUAL] Bristlecone pines near Wheeler Peak are among Earth's oldest living organisms—some individuals exceed 2,000 years, providing living archives of climate history
    [TURNING] The Taos Plateau volcanic field (5-3 MYA) buried older fossil-bearing deposits under 600+ feet of basalt, creating a "basalt barrier" that makes fossils inaccessible
    [TURNING] The Rio Grande Rift initiation (~30-35 MYA) fundamentally reshaped the landscape, creating basin-and-range topography that influenced all subsequent evolution
    [TURNING] Pleistocene glaciers carved cirques and valleys in the highest peaks, creating refugia for cold-adapted species and isolating populations
    [HUMAN] Limited systematic paleontological research has occurred due to challenging terrain and limited exposure—most discoveries await future exploration

    Precambrian (4.6 billion - 538 MYA)¶

    The Missing Record¶

    No Precambrian fossils exist in Taos—the oldest rocks are metamorphic and igneous basement complex that crystallized deep within the Earth's crust, where no life could exist and no fossils could be preserved. [PARADOX]

    Precambrian Basement Complex¶

    The oldest rocks in the Taos region—exposed at Wheeler Peak and highest elevations—date to 1.7-1.8 billion years ago:

    Rock Type Age Origin Fossil Potential
    Metamorphic gneiss 1.7-1.8 BYA Ancient mountain-building None—metamorphism destroyed any evidence
    Granitic plutons 1.7-1.8 BYA Deep crustal melting None—igneous intrusion
    Schist and quartzite Variable Metamorphosed sediments None—original structures erased

    Significance: The exposure of these Precambrian rocks at New Mexico's highest elevations (13,161 feet) provides a window into deep geological time—rocks that formed miles underground are now uplifted over 13,000 feet above sea level. [UNUSUAL] However, this ancient basement creates a "fossil void" for the region's earliest history—if any Precambrian life existed here, its traces were erased by metamorphism and igneous intrusion.

    Paleozoic Era (538 - 252 MYA)¶

    Marine Life in Shallow Seas¶

    During much of the Paleozoic, the Taos region lay beneath shallow tropical seas that periodically advanced and retreated across the continental interior.

    Cambrian through Devonian (538 - 359 MYA)¶

    Global Context: Explosion of marine life; first vertebrates; plants colonize land.

    Taos Context: Limited sedimentary rock exposure from this period; most evidence removed by erosion.

    Life: Shallow marine environments would have supported: - Trilobites (dominant early Paleozoic) - Brachiopods - Crinoids (sea lilies) - Early fish - Marine invertebrates

    Carboniferous Period (359 - 299 MYA)¶

    Pennsylvanian Subperiod (~320-300 MYA): [TURNING]

    This period provides Taos's best Paleozoic fossil potential:

    Deposit Type Location Fossil Potential
    Limestone Sangre de Cristo foothills Marine invertebrates, brachiopods, crinoids
    Shale Foothill exposures Delicate fossils, plant remains
    Coal seams Limited Plant fossils, swamp environments

    Marine Life: - Brachiopods (shelled marine organisms) - Crinoids (echinoderms with stalked bodies) - Corals (reef-building organisms) - Bryozoans (colonial marine animals) - Foraminifera (microscopic shelled protists)

    Ancestral Rocky Mountains (~300 MYA): [TURNING]

    Early mountain-building created uplifts that would later influence modern topography: - Mountains rose, creating terrestrial environments - Erosion from these mountains deposited thick sediment sequences - Most fossil evidence from this period has been removed by subsequent erosion

    Permian Period (299 - 252 MYA)¶

    Global Context: Pangaea assembly; largest mass extinction in Earth's history approaching.

    Taos Context: Transition from marine to terrestrial environments as seas retreated.

    Permian Extinction (252 MYA): [TURNING]

    The "Great Dying" eliminated approximately 90% of marine species and 70% of terrestrial vertebrates. While direct evidence in Taos is limited, this extinction fundamentally reset global ecosystems and would have affected any life present in the region.

    Mesozoic Era (252 - 66 MYA)¶

    The Age of Dinosaurs¶

    The Mesozoic Era—spanning 186 million years—saw the Taos region transition between terrestrial and marine environments. Dinosaurs certainly inhabited the region during this span, yet no dinosaur fossils have been definitively identified in the immediate Taos area. [UNEXPECTED] [PARADOX]

    Triassic Period (252 - 201 MYA)¶

    Global Context: Recovery from Permian extinction; first dinosaurs; Pangaea intact.

    Taos Context: Continental red beds and river deposits indicate terrestrial environments.

    Environments: - River systems - Dune fields - Desert conditions - Floodplains

    Life: - Early dinosaurs (small, bipedal) - Thecodonts (archosaur ancestors) - Early mammals (small, nocturnal) - Plants: cycads, ferns, conifers

    Fossil Potential: Dinosaur tracks, plant fossils, and terrestrial vertebrate remains may exist in Triassic formations, but extensive erosion and limited exposure reduce discovery likelihood.

    Jurassic Period (201 - 145 MYA)¶

    Global Context: Dinosaurs dominant; first birds; Pangaea splitting; extensive shallow seas.

    Taos Context: Terrestrial deposits; colorful badlands; dune fields.

    Life: - Large sauropod dinosaurs (long-necked herbivores) - Theropod dinosaurs (carnivores) - Early birds (Archaeopteryx contemporaries) - Mammals (still small, diverse) - Plants: cycads, ginkgoes, conifers, ferns

    Fossil Potential: Similar to Triassic—potential exists but limited exploration and erosion have prevented discovery.

    Cretaceous Period (145 - 66 MYA) [TURNING]¶

    Global Context: Peak of dinosaur diversity; flowering plants revolutionize ecosystems; high sea levels; K-Pg mass extinction ends the era.

    Taos Context: This period saw dramatic environmental changes:

    Event Timing Significance
    Western Interior Seaway ~100-70 MYA Shallow sea advanced across region
    Coal-forming swamps ~100-70 MYA Rich plant life along shorelines
    Marine/coastal environments Variable Transition between terrestrial and marine
    K-Pg extinction 66 MYA Global ecosystem reset

    Marine Life (during seaway advance): - Marine reptiles (mosasaurs, plesiosaurs) - Ammonites (abundant; went extinct at K-Pg boundary) - Sharks and bony fish - Marine invertebrates

    Terrestrial Life (when exposed): - Tyrannosaurs and other large theropods - Hadrosaurs (duck-billed dinosaurs) - Ceratopsians (horned dinosaurs) - Flowering plants spreading rapidly - Early mammals diversifying

    Regional Context: Nearby regions (San Juan Basin, Colorado Plateau) have rich Cretaceous fossil records, suggesting Taos area likely had similar life forms, even if direct evidence is sparse.

    K-Pg Extinction (66 MYA) [TURNING]¶

    The asteroid impact that ended the Cretaceous killed approximately 75% of species:

    Group Fate
    Non-avian dinosaurs Extinct
    Ammonites Extinct
    Marine reptiles Extinct
    Birds Survived (descendants of dinosaurs)
    Mammals Survived and diversified
    Flowering plants Survived

    No direct evidence of the impact exists in Taos rocks, but the extinction fundamentally reset the evolutionary trajectory, opening ecological niches that mammals would eventually fill.

    Cenozoic Era (66 MYA - Present)¶

    The Cenozoic—the "Age of Mammals"—shaped Taos's modern ecosystems through dramatic climate changes, tectonic transformations, and evolutionary innovations. (See cenozoic-era.md for detailed coverage.)

    Paleogene Period (66 - 23 MYA)¶

    Paleocene Epoch (66 - 56 MYA)¶

    Post-Extinction Recovery: Mammals rapidly diversified to fill vacant ecological niches.

    Taos Context: Laramide Orogeny beginning; mountain-building initiated.

    Eocene Epoch (56 - 34 MYA)¶

    Peak Warmth: Earth's warmest recent epoch—tropical conditions extended to high latitudes.

    Taos Context: Warm, subtropical conditions; forests developing.

    Oligocene Epoch (34 - 23 MYA)¶

    Cooling Begins: Global temperatures dropped; grasslands began spreading.

    Taos Context: Climate cooling; vegetation shifting toward modern communities.

    Neogene Period (23 - 2.6 MYA)¶

    Miocene Epoch (23 - 5 MYA)¶

    Global Context: Grasslands expand; hominids evolving in Africa.

    Taos Context: This epoch saw dramatic developments:

    Event Timing Significance
    Rio Grande Rift initiation ~30-35 MYA Continental extension begins [TURNING]
    Basin formation ~30-25 MYA Taos Valley begins to form
    Climate aridification Throughout Increasingly dry conditions
    Modern ecosystems developing ~15-5 MYA Vegetation shifting toward present-day

    Pliocene Epoch (5 - 2.6 MYA)¶

    Global Context: Climate oscillating; Ice Ages approaching.

    Taos Context: Dramatic volcanic activity:

    Event Timing Significance
    Taos Plateau Volcanic Field 5-3 MYA Massive basalt flows [TURNING]
    Basalt burial 5-3 MYA Older deposits buried under 600+ feet of lava
    Rio Grande Gorge begins ~5 MYA River starts carving through basalt
    Modern topography emerging Throughout Landscape taking current form

    Quaternary Period (2.6 MYA - Present)¶

    Pleistocene Epoch (2.6 MYA - 12,000 years ago) [TURNING]¶

    The Ice Ages: Dramatic climate swings—at least 20 glacial-interglacial cycles.

    Taos Context: - Glaciers carved cirques and valleys in highest peaks - Sea level fluctuations (not directly relevant inland) - Megafauna roamed the region - Vegetation zones shifted dramatically

    Pleistocene Megafauna:

    Though few fossils have been found within Taos proper (the erosive environment destroys bones rapidly), the region would have supported:

    Species Size Diet Fate
    Columbian Mammoth (Mammuthus columbi) 13 ft at shoulder Grazer Extinct ~11,000 BP
    American Mastodon 10 ft at shoulder Browser Extinct ~10,000 BP
    Giant Ground Sloth (Megalonyx) 10 ft tall Browser Extinct ~11,000 BP
    Saber-toothed Cat (Smilodon fatalis) 800 lbs Carnivore Extinct ~10,000 BP
    Dire Wolf (Aenocyon dirus) 150 lbs Carnivore Extinct ~10,000 BP
    Western Horse (Equus occidentalis) Similar to modern Grazer Extinct ~10,000 BP
    Bison (ancestral) Larger than modern Grazer Survived (barely)

    Megafaunal Extinction [TURNING]:

    The Pleistocene megafauna disappeared approximately 10,000-12,000 years ago:

    Hypothesis Evidence
    Human hunting Timing coincides with human arrival
    Climate change End of Ice Age changed habitats
    Combination Both factors likely contributed

    Holocene Epoch (12,000 years ago - Present)¶

    The Modern Era: Current interglacial period—relative climate stability enabled human civilization.

    Taos Context: - Modern ecosystems established - Glaciers retreated to highest cirques - Human arrival (~12,000-15,000 years ago) [TURNING] - Taos Pueblo established (~1,000 years ago) [HUMAN] - European contact (1540) initiated modern era

    Endemic Lineages and Living Fossils¶

    Bristlecone Pine (Pinus longaeva or Pinus aristata) [UNUSUAL]¶

    Bristlecone pines near Wheeler Peak are among Earth's oldest living organisms:

    Attribute Value
    Age Some individuals exceed 2,000 years
    Distribution High elevations (10,000-12,000+ ft)
    Lineage Age Genus dates to Cretaceous (~100 MYA)
    Surviving Species 3 species worldwide; 1-2 in region
    Why It Survived Here High-elevation refugia; slow growth; extreme longevity

    Evolutionary Story: Bristlecone pines represent an ancient conifer lineage that has persisted through dramatic climate changes. Their extreme longevity—some individuals exceed 2,000 years—makes them living archives of climate history. The harsh high-elevation conditions that limit most life actually favor these slow-growing trees, allowing them to outcompete faster-growing species and persist for millennia.

    Climate History and Ecosystem Evolution¶

    Long-term Climate Trends¶

    Period Climate Ecosystems
    Paleozoic Warm, tropical seas Marine ecosystems
    Mesozoic Warm, variable Terrestrial forests; marine seaways
    Early Cenozoic Warm ("hothouse") Subtropical forests
    Mid-Cenozoic Cooling begins Temperate forests; grasslands
    Late Cenozoic Aridification Desert/semi-desert; montane forests
    Pleistocene Ice Age cycles Dramatic fluctuations
    Holocene Current interglacial Modern high-elevation ecosystems

    Glacial Cycles [TURNING]¶

    The Pleistocene brought dramatic climate swings:

    Condition Glacial Period Interglacial Period
    Ice Extent Glaciers in highest cirques Glaciers retreated
    Climate Cold, often dry Warm, variable
    Vegetation Zones Lowered; compressed Similar to present
    Sea Level Lower (not directly relevant) Higher

    Glacial Features: - Cirques carved in highest peaks - Moraines marking ice extent - U-shaped valleys - Glacial striations on bedrock - Multiple advances and retreats documented

    Elevation and Life Zone Development¶

    The extreme elevation gradient in Taos created compressed life zones:

    Zone Elevation Development Timing
    Alpine Tundra 12,000-13,161 ft Pleistocene-Holocene
    Subalpine Forest 11,000-12,000 ft Pleistocene
    Montane Forest 8,000-11,000 ft Miocene-Pleistocene
    Piñon-Juniper 7,000-8,500 ft Pliocene-Pleistocene
    High Desert 6,200-7,500 ft Pliocene-Pleistocene

    This compressed zonation—spanning life zones equivalent to traveling from northern Mexico to arctic Canada—developed primarily during the Cenozoic as mountains rose and climate cooled. [UNUSUAL]

    Fossil Sites and Paleontological Research¶

    Known Fossil Sites¶

    Paleozoic Marine Fossils: - Pennsylvanian limestone and shale exposures in Sangre de Cristo foothills - Potential for marine invertebrate fossils (brachiopods, crinoids, corals) - Limited systematic collection has occurred

    Potential Mesozoic Sites: - Triassic-Jurassic red beds have fossil potential but remain largely unexplored - Cretaceous sandstones may contain marine/terrestrial fossils - Regional context suggests dinosaur fossils likely exist but await discovery

    Quaternary Megafauna: - Ice Age mammal fossils potentially preserved in alluvial deposits - Limited documented discoveries; most evidence is indirect

    Research History [HUMAN]¶

    Systematic paleontological research in the Taos region has been limited compared to other areas of New Mexico:

    Period Activity Status
    Early 1900s Geological surveys Basic rock mapping
    Mid-1900s Oil/gas exploration Some subsurface data
    Late 1900s Limited fossil collection Incidental discoveries
    2000s-present Ongoing but sparse Most discoveries await future exploration

    Challenges: - Challenging terrain (steep mountains, remote locations) - Limited exposure of fossil-bearing rocks - Dominance of non-fossiliferous rock types - Volcanic burial of older deposits

    Current Research¶

    Active Areas: - Glacial geology and chronology - Tree-ring climate reconstruction - Pollen analysis (limited) - Living fossil studies

    Unanswered Questions: - Where are the dinosaur fossils? - What megafauna fossils exist in alluvium? - How far back do tree-ring records extend? - What do lake sediment cores reveal?
