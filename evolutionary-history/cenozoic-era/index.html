    Cenozoic Era in Taos, New Mexico¶

    Era Overview¶

    Field Value
    Era Cenozoic ("Recent Life")
    Time Range 66 MYA - Present
    Duration 66 million years
    Global Context Age of Mammals; continents approach modern positions; climate cools
    Regional Significance Formation of modern ecosystems; Rio Grande Rift begins; Taos takes shape

    The Cenozoic is Taos's defining era—the time when the region acquired its distinctive character. The Rio Grande Rift initiated, creating basin-and-range topography. The Taos Plateau volcanic field erupted, burying older deposits under basalt. Glaciers carved cirques and valleys in the highest peaks. Mammals diversified and megafauna roamed the landscape. And ultimately, the high desert valley we know today emerged from a landscape transformed by tectonics, volcanism, and climate change.

    Narrative Highlights¶

    Flag Evolutionary Story
    [TURNING] Rio Grande Rift initiation (~30-35 MYA) fundamentally reshaped the landscape, creating basin-and-range topography
    [TURNING] Taos Plateau Volcanic Field (5-3 MYA) buried older fossil-bearing deposits under 600+ feet of basalt
    [TURNING] Pleistocene glaciation (2.6 MYA - 12,000 BP) carved cirques and valleys, creating refugia for cold-adapted species
    [TURNING] Megafaunal extinction (~10,000-12,000 BP) eliminated mammoths, mastodons, saber-toothed cats, and giant sloths
    [PARADOX] Despite abundant Ice Age megafauna likely present, fossils are rare due to erosion and volcanic burial
    [HUMAN] First humans arrived ~12,000-15,000 years ago, fundamentally altering ecosystems

    Paleogene Period (66 - 23 MYA)¶

    Paleocene Epoch (66 - 56 MYA)¶

    Post-Extinction Recovery

    The Paleocene began in the aftermath of the K-Pg mass extinction. With dinosaurs gone, mammals rapidly diversified to fill vacant ecological niches.

    Global Context: - Recovery from extinction - Mammals diversifying - Warm global climate - Tropical to subtropical conditions

    Taos Context: - Laramide Orogeny beginning (~70-40 MYA) - Mountain-building initiated - Warm, subtropical conditions - Forests developing - No terrestrial fossils known from this time

    Mammalian Radiation:

    Group Development
    Primitive mammals Small, diverse forms
    Early ungulates Hoofed mammal ancestors
    Primates Early representatives
    Carnivores Early forms

    Eocene Epoch (56 - 34 MYA)¶

    Peak Warmth

    The Eocene was Earth's warmest recent epoch—palm trees grew in Alaska, and no permanent ice existed on the planet.

    Climate: - Global temperatures 10-15°F warmer than today - Tropical and subtropical conditions extended to high latitudes - No polar ice caps - Sea levels higher than present

    Taos Context: - Warm, subtropical conditions - Forests dominated by broadleaf trees - Laramide Orogeny continuing - Sangre de Cristo Mountains beginning to rise

    Life: - Diverse mammals (still relatively small) - Early horses (dog-sized) - Primitive carnivores - Rodents diversifying - Bats evolving flight

    Oligocene Epoch (34 - 23 MYA)¶

    Cooling Begins [TURNING]

    The Oligocene marked a fundamental climate shift: - Antarctic ice sheet formed - Global temperatures dropped - Grasslands began spreading - Ocean circulation patterns changed

    Tectonic Significance:

    The Rio Grande Rift initiated during the late Oligocene (~30-35 MYA): [TURNING]

    Before Rio Grande Rift After Rio Grande Rift
    Stable continental interior Continental extension begins
    No basin formation Taos Valley begins to form
    Compression (mountain-building) Extension (rift formation)
    Single tectonic regime Transitional setting

    This tectonic transformation would ultimately create the basin-and-range topography that defines modern Taos.

    Life Developments: - Grasslands spreading - Grazing mammals evolving - Climate cooling - Vegetation shifting toward modern communities

    Neogene Period (23 - 2.6 MYA)¶

    Miocene Epoch (23 - 5 MYA)¶

    The Age of Modernization

    The Miocene saw the development of recognizably modern ecosystems and the dramatic volcanic activity that would bury older deposits.

    Global Context: - Grasslands expand globally - Modern mammal families establish - Hominids evolving in Africa - Climate continuing to cool

    Taos Context: This epoch saw dramatic developments:

    Event Timing Significance
    Rio Grande Rift active Throughout Basin deepening; extension continuing
    Taos Valley formation ~20-10 MYA Valley taking shape
    Climate aridification Throughout Increasingly dry conditions
    Modern ecosystems developing ~15-5 MYA Vegetation shifting toward present-day
    Volcanic activity begins ~10-5 MYA Precursor to major volcanism

    Mammalian Diversity:

    Group Development
    Horses Larger forms; grazing adaptations
    Camelids Diverse; some large forms
    Rhinoceroses Present in region
    Deer Diversifying
    Carnivores Modern families appearing
    Rodents Highly diverse

    Vegetation Changes: - Grasslands expanding - Conifers spreading southward as climate cooled - Chaparral communities developing - Desert/semi-desert vegetation increasing

    Pliocene Epoch (5 - 2.6 MYA)¶

    Approaching the Modern World

    The Pliocene saw continued cooling and dramatic volcanic activity that would fundamentally alter the Taos landscape.

    Climate Trends: - Global temperatures declining - Ice sheets expanding - Seasonal climate strengthening - Aridification continuing

    Taos Developments:

    Process Effect
    Mountain Uplift Sangre de Cristo Mountains rising rapidly
    Valley Deepening Rio Grande Rift continuing
    Climate Cooling Vegetation zones shifting
    Volcanic Activity Major basalt flows beginning

    Taos Plateau Volcanic Field (5-3 MYA) [TURNING]:

    This was Taos's most dramatic Pliocene event:

    Attribute Value
    Duration ~2 million years
    Volume Hundreds of cubic miles of basalt
    Thickness 600+ feet in places
    Coverage Much of Taos Valley and plateau
    Effect Buried older fossil-bearing deposits

    The Basalt Barrier:

    The volcanic flows created what we might call "The Basalt Barrier"—older sedimentary deposits that might contain fossils are now buried under hundreds of feet of impermeable basalt, making them inaccessible without deep drilling or extensive erosion. [PARADOX]

    Life During Pliocene: - Modern mammal genera appearing - Megafauna developing (precursors to Ice Age giants) - Vegetation similar to present-day - Climate becoming increasingly variable

    Quaternary Period (2.6 MYA - Present)¶

    Pleistocene Epoch (2.6 MYA - 11,700 years ago)¶

    The Ice Ages [TURNING]

    The Pleistocene brought dramatic climate swings—at least 20 glacial-interglacial cycles:

    Condition Glacial Period Interglacial Period
    Ice Extent Glaciers in highest cirques Glaciers retreated
    Climate Cold, often dry Warm, variable
    Vegetation Zones Lowered; compressed Similar to present
    Precipitation Variable; often reduced More consistent

    Big Sur During Ice Ages:

    Feature Glacial Conditions Interglacial Conditions
    High Peaks Glaciers present Ice-free except permanent snowfields
    Vegetation Lowered elevation zones Similar to present
    Megafauna Present; mammoth, sloth, cats Present until ~10,000 BP
    Human Presence Arriving late Pleistocene Established by Holocene

    Glacial Features:

    Multiple glacial advances carved the high country:

    Feature Location Significance
    Cirques Wheeler Peak area Ice-carved amphitheaters
    Moraines Valley floors Mark ice extent
    U-shaped Valleys High canyons Glacial erosion
    Glacial Striations Bedrock surfaces Ice movement direction
    Tarns Cirque floors Glacial lakes

    Pleistocene Megafauna [TURNING]:

    Pleistocene Taos hosted spectacular large mammals:

    Species Weight Habitat Diet Extinction
    Columbian Mammoth (Mammuthus columbi) 20,000 lbs Grasslands, open woodland Grazer ~11,000 BP
    American Mastodon 12,000 lbs Forests, wetlands Browser ~10,000 BP
    Giant Ground Sloth (Megalonyx) 4,000 lbs Varied Browser ~11,000 BP
    Saber-toothed Cat (Smilodon fatalis) 800 lbs Varied Carnivore ~10,000 BP
    Dire Wolf (Aenocyon dirus) 150 lbs Packs Carnivore ~10,000 BP
    Short-faced Bear (Arctodus) 2,000 lbs Varied Omnivore ~11,000 BP
    Western Horse (Equus occidentalis) 800 lbs Grasslands Grazer ~10,000 BP
    Western Camel (Camelops) 1,500 lbs Varied Browser/grazer ~10,000 BP
    Bison (ancestral) Larger than modern Grasslands Grazer Survived (barely)

    Why Megafauna Fossils Are Rare [PARADOX]:

    Despite abundant megafauna likely present, fossils are rare in Taos due to: - Erosive mountain environment destroys bones rapidly - Volcanic basalt covers potential burial sites - Limited alluvial deposits for preservation - Steep terrain sheds remains downslope - Most evidence is indirect (from nearby regions)

    Megafaunal Extinction (~10,000-12,000 BP): [TURNING]

    All of these species disappeared at the end of the Pleistocene:

    Hypothesis Evidence For Evidence Against
    Human Hunting Timing coincides with human arrival Not all kills are near human sites
    Climate Change End of Ice Age changed habitats Previous climate changes didn't cause extinction
    Combination Multiple stressors together Difficult to disentangle

    The disappearance of megafauna fundamentally altered Taos's ecosystems—without mammoths and ground sloths, vegetation patterns changed dramatically.

    Holocene Epoch (11,700 years ago - Present)¶

    The Modern Era

    The Holocene represents the current interglacial period—a time of relative climate stability that enabled human civilization to develop:

    Climate Stabilization: - Glaciers retreated to highest cirques - Modern vegetation zones developed - Fire regime established - Climate relatively stable (until recent warming)

    Human Arrival [TURNING]:

    Event Timing Significance
    First Humans in Americas ~15,000-20,000 BP Via Beringia or coastal route
    Human Presence in Region ~12,000-15,000 BP First known inhabitants
    Taos Pueblo Established ~1,000 BP Continuous habitation [UNUSUAL]
    European Contact 1540 Spanish arrival
    Modern Era 1800s-present Anglo settlement; art colony

    Ecological Establishment:

    Modern Taos ecosystems took shape:

    Ecosystem Establishment Key Species
    Alpine Tundra Throughout Holocene Grasses, sedges, cushion plants
    Subalpine Forest Throughout Holocene Engelmann spruce, subalpine fir
    Montane Forest Throughout Holocene Ponderosa pine, Douglas-fir
    Piñon-Juniper Throughout Holocene Piñon pine, Utah juniper
    High Desert Throughout Holocene Sagebrush, grasses

    Modern Ecosystem Development¶

    Elevation Zonation¶

    The extreme elevation gradient in Taos creates compressed life zones:

    Zone Elevation Development Key Factors
    Alpine Tundra 12,000-13,161 ft Pleistocene-Holocene Glacial refugia; cold adaptation
    Subalpine Forest 11,000-12,000 ft Pleistocene Cold-adapted conifers
    Montane Forest 8,000-11,000 ft Miocene-Pleistocene Cool, moist conditions
    Piñon-Juniper 7,000-8,500 ft Pliocene-Pleistocene Aridification
    High Desert 6,200-7,500 ft Pliocene-Pleistocene Increasing aridity

    This compressed zonation—spanning life zones equivalent to traveling from northern Mexico to arctic Canada—developed primarily during the Cenozoic as mountains rose and climate cooled. [UNUSUAL]

    Glacial Refugia¶

    Pleistocene glaciers created refugia for cold-adapted species:

    Refugium Type Location Species
    North-facing Cirques Wheeler Peak area Permanent snowfields; ice-dependent ecosystems
    High Elevations Above 12,000 ft Alpine specialists
    Protected Valleys Glacial valleys Cold-adapted plants and animals

    These refugia allowed species to persist through climate changes, creating isolated populations that may have led to speciation.

    Fire Regime Development¶

    Fire has shaped Taos ecosystems throughout the Holocene:

    Fire Type Frequency Effect
    Lightning fires Natural Maintained open forests
    Human-set fires Variable Indigenous management
    Modern suppression Recent Altered fire patterns

    Fire regime changes have influenced vegetation composition and structure throughout the Holocene.
