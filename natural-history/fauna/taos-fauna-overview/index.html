    Taos, New Mexico Fauna Overview¶

    Basic Information¶

    Field Value
    Total Vertebrate Species ~300+ (estimated)
    Mammal Species ~60-70 species
    Bird Species ~200+ species (resident and migratory)
    Reptile Species ~15-20 species
    Amphibian Species ~5-10 species
    Fish Species ~10-15 species (native and introduced)
    Endemic Species/Subspecies Rio Grande cutthroat trout (subspecies)
    Threatened Species Several species of concern; recovery successes for some species
    Elevation Range 6,200 to 13,161 feet (Wheeler Peak)

    Introduction¶

    Taos, New Mexico's fauna reflects the region's dramatic elevation gradient—nearly 7,000 feet from valley floor to Wheeler Peak's summit at 13,161 feet. This extreme vertical range creates exceptional wildlife diversity, with species adapted to environments ranging from high desert valleys to alpine tundra. [UNUSUAL] The compressed life zones enable wildlife to move seasonally between elevations, while the region's relative isolation has allowed some species to develop unique adaptations.

    The region's wildlife tells stories of adaptation, persistence, and human connection. [HUMAN] Indigenous peoples have maintained deep relationships with regional wildlife for millennia, with traditional knowledge of animal behavior, ecology, and sustainable use practices. [TURNING] Historical extinctions—including grizzly bear, gray wolf, and bison—altered ecosystem dynamics, while successful reintroductions of elk and wild turkey represent conservation victories. The native Rio Grande cutthroat trout faces ongoing conservation challenges, while high-elevation specialists like pika demonstrate remarkable adaptations to extreme conditions.

    Mammals¶

    Overview¶

    Mammal diversity in Taos reflects the region's elevation gradient, with species adapted to environments from high desert to alpine tundra. [UNUSUAL] The compressed life zones enable seasonal movements between elevations, while historical extinctions have reduced diversity from pre-European levels.

    Species Count: ~60-70 species in multiple families

    Large Mammals¶

    The Taos region supports several large mammal species, though historical extinctions have reduced diversity:

    • Elk (Cervus canadensis) - Large ungulates, successfully reintroduced and now thriving in montane forests [TURNING]
    • Mule deer (Odocoileus hemionus) - Common throughout, from valley to forest zones
    • Pronghorn (Antilocapra americana) - Open grassland and sagebrush specialists, found in valley areas
    • Bighorn sheep (Ovis canadensis) - Rocky mountain specialists, historically present, conservation status varies
    • Black bear (Ursus americanus) - Forest-dwelling omnivores, present in montane zones
    • Mountain lion (Puma concolor) - Apex predator, present but rarely seen [UNUSUAL]

    Historical Losses: [TURNING] - Grizzly bear - Extirpated by early 1900s, last recorded kill in New Mexico mountains - Gray wolf - Extirpated, though occasional dispersers from northern populations may pass through - Bison - Historically present on plains, extirpated from region

    Medium and Small Mammals¶

    • Coyote (Canis latrans) - Highly adaptable canid, common throughout
    • Red fox (Vulpes vulpes) - Present in forested areas
    • Bobcat (Lynx rufus) - Forest-dwelling predator
    • Porcupine (Erethizon dorsatum) - Forest specialist, notable for quills
    • Beaver (Castor canadensis) - Riparian engineer, present along streams; historically important for fur trade [HUMAN]
    • Pika (Ochotona princeps) - Alpine specialist, found above treeline [UNUSUAL]
    • Various rodents - Squirrels, chipmunks, mice, voles adapted to different elevations
    • Bats - Multiple species, important insectivores

    Birds¶

    Overview¶

    Bird diversity in Taos includes both resident and migratory species, with high-elevation specialists and species adapted to various habitats. [UNUSUAL] The region's elevation gradient supports species typically found in widely separated regions.

    Species Count: ~200+ species in multiple families Resident Species: ~100+ species Migratory Species: ~100+ species (seasonal)

    Raptors¶

    • Golden eagle (Aquila chrysaetos) - Large raptor, nests on cliffs, hunts across elevations
    • Bald eagle (Haliaeetus leucocephalus) - Present along rivers, especially during winter
    • Red-tailed hawk (Buteo jamaicensis) - Common throughout, most frequently seen raptor
    • Peregrine falcon (Falco peregrinus) - Cliff-nesting specialist, recovery success story from DDT impacts [TURNING]
    • Northern goshawk (Accipiter gentilis) - Forest-dwelling predator

    Songbirds and Other Birds¶

    • Steller's jay (Cyanocitta stelleri) - Montane forest corvid, intelligent and vocal
    • Clark's nutcracker (Nucifraga columbiana) - High-elevation specialist, important seed disperser for conifers [UNUSUAL]
    • Mountain bluebird (Sialia currucoides) - Open country specialist
    • Various warblers - Migratory species passing through during spring and fall
    • Hummingbirds - Multiple species, important pollinators, attracted to high-elevation flowers
    • Woodpeckers - Multiple species including downy, hairy, and three-toed woodpeckers

    Game Birds¶

    • Wild turkey (Meleagris gallopavo) - Reintroduced, now common in forests [TURNING]
    • Blue grouse (Dendragapus obscurus) - High-elevation forest specialist
    • Various quail - Scaled quail and Gambel's quail in lower elevations

    Waterfowl¶

    • Mallard (Anas platyrhynchos) - Common along rivers and ponds
    • Canada goose (Branta canadensis) - Present along waterways
    • Various ducks - Seasonal migrants and residents

    Reptiles and Amphibians¶

    Reptiles¶

    The high elevation and cold winters limit reptile diversity compared to lower-elevation desert regions:

    • Gopher snake (Pituophis catenifer) - Common non-venomous snake
    • Prairie rattlesnake (Crotalus viridis) - Venomous snake, present but less common at high elevations
    • Various lizards - Including fence lizards and other small species
    • Limited diversity: Cold winters and high elevation restrict reptile populations

    Species Count: ~15-20 species

    Amphibians¶

    • Boreal chorus frog (Pseudacris maculata) - Small frog, breeds in temporary pools
    • Tiger salamander (Ambystoma tigrinum) - Present in suitable aquatic habitats
    • Limited diversity: Arid conditions and high elevation limit amphibian populations

    Species Count: ~5-10 species

    Fish and Aquatic Species¶

    Freshwater Fish¶

    Native Species: - Rio Grande cutthroat trout (Oncorhynchus clarkii virginalis) - Native trout subspecies, conservation priority [TURNING] [UNUSUAL] - Rio Grande sucker (Catostomus plebeius) - Native sucker species - Various minnows - Native small fish species

    Introduced Species: - Rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) - Widely introduced for sport fishing - Brown trout (Salmo trutta) - Introduced, now common in many streams - Brook trout (Salvelinus fontinalis) - Introduced to high-elevation streams

    Conservation Challenges: Native fish face threats from introduced species, habitat alteration, and water diversions. Rio Grande cutthroat trout is a conservation priority with ongoing recovery efforts.

    Species Count: ~10-15 species (native and introduced)

    Origins and Evolution¶

    While Taos lacks many strict endemics, the Rio Grande cutthroat trout represents an important regional subspecies. [UNUSUAL] This native trout subspecies has evolved unique adaptations to southwestern streams, representing a distinct evolutionary lineage within the cutthroat trout complex.

    Regional Specialties¶

    Rio Grande Cutthroat Trout (Oncorhynchus clarkii virginalis): [UNUSUAL] - Native trout subspecies found in Rio Grande drainage - Conservation priority due to habitat loss and competition from introduced trout - Represents unique evolutionary lineage adapted to southwestern streams

    High-Elevation Specialists: - Pika - Alpine mammal surviving in harsh conditions above treeline [UNUSUAL] - Clark's nutcracker - Important seed disperser for high-elevation conifers [UNUSUAL] - Various alpine species - Adapted to extreme high-elevation conditions

    Conservation of Specialties¶

    Rio Grande cutthroat trout faces ongoing conservation challenges from introduced species, habitat alteration, and water diversions. [TURNING] High-elevation specialists face increasing threats from climate change as suitable habitat shrinks.

    Seasonal Patterns¶

    Wildlife Calendar¶

    Season Wildlife Events
    Early Spring (March-April) Migratory birds arrive; elk move to lower elevations; breeding begins
    Late Spring (May-June) Peak breeding season; young of year appear; migratory birds nesting
    Summer (July-August) Peak activity; young of year growing; high-elevation species active during brief season
    Fall (September-October) Elk rut (breeding season); migration begins; preparation for winter
    Winter (November-February) Hibernation; winter residents; elk in lower elevations; limited activity at high elevations

    Migration¶

    Migratory patterns include bird migrations through the region, seasonal elevation movements by mammals, and fish movements within waterways. [UNUSUAL] The region's elevation gradient enables seasonal movements between elevations, with species moving upslope in summer and downslope in winter.

    Climate Change Impacts [TURNING]¶

    Observed Changes¶

    High-elevation species are already experiencing impacts from climate change, with warming temperatures affecting species distributions, phenology, and habitat availability. [TURNING] Alpine communities face particular challenges as suitable habitat shrinks.

    Vulnerable Species¶

    High-elevation specialists, including pika, Clark's nutcracker, and alpine-adapted species, are most vulnerable to climate change. [TURNING] These species have limited options for upward migration as temperatures warm, potentially facing local extinctions.

    Range Shifts¶

    Climate models project continued warming, which will likely: - Affect species distributions along elevation gradients - Alter migration patterns and timing - Impact high-elevation species most severely - Create new challenges for conservation
